Movement & Training

The Building Blocks of Dance

Adavus are the fundamental units of movement in Bharatanatyam — the syllables of its kinetic language. Mastering these combinations of steps, postures and rhythms forms the foundation of technical proficiency.

01

What are Adavus?

Adavus (pronounced "ah-dah-vus") are the basic choreographic units or "steps" that form the vocabulary of Bharatanatyam. Each adavu combines specific movements of the legs, feet, arms, and torso with rhythmic patterns, creating a systematic approach to learning and performing the dance.

Traditionally, there are 108 adavus, though modern teaching often focuses on the most essential ones. These are practiced repeatedly to develop muscle memory, balance, coordination, and rhythmic precision.

02

Types of Adavus

Adavus are categorized based on their dominant movement patterns and rhythmic structures. The main categories include:

  • Tattu Adavus: Basic stamping movements
  • Nattu Adavus: Sliding and gliding steps
  • Kuthu Adavus: Jumping and leaping movements
  • Mandi Adavus: Squatting and rising patterns
  • Sarikal Adavus: Sliding movements with turns

Key Elements

  • Precise foot placement
  • Arm positions (hastas)
  • Body alignment
  • Rhythmic accuracy
  • Graceful transitions
03

Training & Practice

Adavu practice begins with learning the basic positions and movements, then progresses to combining them into sequences. Students typically start with simple adavus and gradually incorporate more complex combinations as their technique develops.

Regular practice builds strength, flexibility, and musicality. The repetitive nature of adavu training helps internalize the rhythmic patterns and movement vocabulary that form the basis of all Bharatanatyam choreography.

04

Rhythm & Tala

Each adavu is performed to specific rhythmic cycles called talas. The most common talas used in adavu practice are Adi Tala (8 beats) and Rupaka Tala (6 beats). The movements are synchronized with the bols (syllables) of the rhythm, creating a seamless integration of dance and music.

Understanding tala is essential for adavu practice, as the timing and accents of the movements must align precisely with the musical structure.
