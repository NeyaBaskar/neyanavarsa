Abhinaya & Emotion

The Language of Expression

Abhinaya is where Bharatanatyam breathes — where stories, poetry and inner feeling are translated into glance, gesture and stillness. This page traces how expression is understood, trained and experienced.

01

Meaning of Abhinaya

Abhinaya literally means “to carry towards” — carrying the inner idea of a poem, story or emotion towards the audience. In Bharatanatyam it is the art of revealing what the character feels, thinks and longs for.

Expression is not an ornament added at the end; it is the pulse of the form. Through abhinaya, the dancer transforms technique into lived experience, inviting the viewer into an intimate imaginative space.

Why expression matters

  • Connects narrative, rhythm and melody
  • Makes the divine and mythic feel personal
  • Turns movement into shared emotional experience
02

Types of Abhinaya (Natya Shastra)

Angika Abhinaya

Expression through the physical body. In Bharatanatyam this includes stance, lines of the limbs, and the dynamic quality of movement.

  • Body movements and overall posture
  • Facial expressions shaped by jaw, cheeks and brows
  • Hand gestures (hasta) that symbolise words or ideas
  • Codified eye, neck and head movements

Vachika Abhinaya

Expression through sound: lyrics, spoken word and rhythmic syllables. Even when the dancer is silent, vachika surrounds them.

  • Poetic sahitya that carries meaning
  • Musical phrases that colour emotion
  • Recitation and rhythmic calls of the nattuvanar
  • Subtle use of breath and sighs in performance

Aharya Abhinaya

Expression through costume, jewellery, makeup and stage design. These elements frame the dancer as a living icon.

  • Costume lines that highlight movement and stance
  • Makeup emphasising eyes and facial structure
  • Ornaments that signal character, role or mood
  • Stage elements that support the atmosphere

Satvika Abhinaya

Expression through inner, involuntary signs of feeling — when the dancer genuinely experiences the emotion of the role.

  • Tears, trembling and changes in breath
  • Softening or tightening of the face
  • Subtle shifts of weight and stillness
  • Authentic psychological presence on stage
03

Navarasas — Nine Emotions

Classical aesthetics speaks of nine primary rasas or flavours of emotion. In Bharatanatyam, abhinaya invites the audience to taste each one through gesture, rhythm and stillness.

Shringara — Love
Hasya — Joy
Karuna — Compassion
Raudra — Anger
Veera — Courage
Bhayanaka — Fear
Bibhatsa — Disgust
Adbhuta — Wonder
Shanta — Peace
04

Bhava and Rasa

Bhava is the inner emotional state of the dancer or character. Rasa is the refined flavour of that emotion savoured by the audience.

The dancer’s responsibility is not only to feel but to communicate. Through carefully crafted abhinaya, bhava travels outward and becomes rasa in the sensitive viewer, creating a shared aesthetic experience.

Dancer & audience

Abhinaya completes a circuit: emotion arises in the dancer, travels through gesture and gaze, and is received as rasa in the heart of the rasika.

05

Facial Expressions

The face is a microcosm of the story. Through minute changes in eyes, eyebrows, cheeks and lips, a dancer can move from delight to doubt in a heartbeat.

Eyes, Brows & Cheeks

  • Eye movements (drishti bheda) that follow rhythm and narrative
  • Eyebrow lifts, arches and knots that suggest subtle thought
  • Cheek and jaw tension that hint at unsaid feeling

Lips & Timing

  • Half-smiles, sighs and parted lips for surprise
  • Synchronization with tala so expressions land on the beat
  • Controlled stillness between phrases to let emotion settle
06

Expression Through Eyes (Drishti)

Eye movements guide where the audience should look and how they should feel about what they see. A single shift of gaze can transform respect into mischief or fear.

Different types of drishti trace paths — up, down, sideward, circling or darting — each carrying its own emotional suggestion and rhythm.

Storytelling with the eyes

In abhinaya, the eyes can describe distance, recall memory, search for the beloved or invite the audience into a shared secret.

07

Sanchari Bhavas

Sanchari bhavas are the fleeting, transitional emotions that appear while moving from one dominant mood to another — curiosity, hesitation, sudden doubt, quiet joy.

They prevent a performance from feeling flat, revealing the inner thought process of a character and making stories feel layered and believable.

08

Role of Abhinaya in Repertoire

In a traditional margam, abhinaya deepens as the programme unfolds. While pure dance pieces foreground rhythm and geometry, later items dwell in expression.

  • Varnam — interweaves rigorous footwork with intense, repeated emotional exploration.
  • Padam & Javalli — slower, intimate pieces that focus almost entirely on nuanced abhinaya.
  • Other compositions use brief abhinaya passages to sketch character and context.

Expression as climax

Many audiences remember a performance most vividly for a single abhinaya moment — a glance, a pause, a quiet smile that lingers long after the rhythm has faded.

09

Training in Abhinaya

Abhinaya is cultivated slowly. Beyond memorising gestures, dancers learn to observe people, absorb poetry and sit with their own emotions with honesty.

Practice methods

  • Working in front of a mirror to refine clarity
  • Using imagination to “see” scene partners, places and deities
  • Repeating lines of poetry until they feel internalised

Role of the Guru

  • Guiding emotional discipline and boundaries
  • Demonstrating nuance that cannot be written down
  • Helping the dancer find their own authentic voice
10

Significance of Abhinaya in Bharatanatyam

Through abhinaya, Bharatanatyam becomes more than choreography; it becomes a meeting place between dancer, audience, culture and the sacred.

Expression allows stories and philosophies to be felt rather than only understood. It forges connection across language, geography and time, revealing why this classical form continues to move people around the world.
